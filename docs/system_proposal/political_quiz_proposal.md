# The Governance Compass

## A Comprehensive, Country-Agnostic Political Belief System Assessment

### Design Proposal v1

---

## 1. Design Philosophy

Every popular political quiz fails in the same fundamental way: it asks people what they believe in a vacuum, then plots the answers on axes derived from Western European parliamentary history. The result is a tool that feels illuminating but measures poorly, validates nothing, and breaks down the moment you leave the Anglosphere.

This instrument takes a different approach. It measures **governance preferences** — how a person believes a nation-state should be organized, who should hold power, how resources should be allocated, and what tradeoffs are acceptable — rather than policy positions tied to any particular country's debates. It uses question formats drawn from experimental political science rather than opinion polling. And it is designed from the ground up for cross-cultural validity, using abstract governance tensions that appear in every society rather than concepts that carry specific cultural freight.

The name, **The Governance Compass**, reflects the shift in framing. This is not about where you sit on a left-right spectrum. It's about what kind of state you would build if you could build one from scratch.

### Core Design Principles

**Tradeoffs over agreement.** Real governance is about choosing between competing goods under constraint. The primary question format forces choices between two defensible positions rather than asking for agreement with a single statement. This eliminates acquiescence bias (empirically shown to inflate attitude estimates by up to 50%) and mirrors how political decisions actually work.

**Revealed preferences over stated preferences.** A person who claims to prioritize the environment but, when forced to allocate scarce resources, consistently funds economic growth over ecological protection has revealed something their stated beliefs obscure. The instrument includes mechanisms that surface these contradictions without judgment.

**Universality through abstraction.** Questions reference "the state," "the population," "neighboring nations" — never specific countries, parties, leaders, or policies. Concepts like "free speech" or "universal healthcare" are decomposed into their underlying governance tensions (individual expression vs. social cohesion; collective risk-pooling vs. individual responsibility) to avoid terms that carry different legal and cultural meanings across societies.

**Dimensional honesty.** Political science converges on two robust primary dimensions (economic and cultural) with strong evidence for 4–8 additional meaningful axes. The instrument measures 12 dimensions — enough to capture genuine complexity, few enough to avoid respondent fatigue and spurious precision. Dimensions are organized hierarchically: two super-dimensions subsume more granular sub-axes, allowing both a simple compass-style overview and detailed drill-down.

## 2. Dimensional Architecture

The 12 axes are organized into four domains. Each axis is defined by two poles that represent genuine, defensible positions — neither pole is inherently pejorative.

### Domain A: Economic Organization

**Axis 1 — Collective Provision ↔ Market Allocation**

The foundational economic divide. One pole holds that organized society bears responsibility for ensuring baseline welfare, redistributing resources, and constraining the accumulation of private power. The other holds that voluntary exchange, private ownership, and price signals produce better outcomes than central planning, and that the state's economic role should be minimal.

This is the single most empirically robust dimension in political science, appearing in every major framework from CHES to the World Values Survey to Moral Foundations Theory (where it maps onto Care/Fairness vs. Liberty).

**Axis 2 — Ecological Limits ↔ Growth Imperative**

Decoupled from the question of *who controls the economy*, this axis asks *how much economic activity is desirable*. One pole holds that continuous aggregate growth on a finite planet is physically impossible and that governance must prioritize sustainability, sufficiency, and ecological boundaries even at the cost of reduced material output. The other holds that growth — whether achieved through green technology, market incentives, or state investment — remains essential for human flourishing and that innovation can decouple prosperity from environmental degradation.

This axis is absent from most existing quizzes but represents a genuine ideological fault line that cross-cuts the traditional left-right economic axis. A committed socialist and a committed capitalist can both be productivist; a degrowth advocate and a green-tech optimist can both care deeply about the environment but disagree on the fundamental question of limits.

### Domain B: Power and Authority

**Axis 3 — Distributed Governance ↔ Centralized Governance**

How should political power be structured geographically and institutionally? One pole favors maximum devolution — local autonomy, federated structures, subsidiarity, direct participation. The other favors unified national authority — standardized law, centralized administration, top-down coordination. This axis is independent of *who* holds power (democratic or otherwise); a centralized democracy and a centralized autocracy both sit on the same pole here, but for different reasons.

**Axis 4 — Popular Sovereignty ↔ Institutional Authority**

Where does legitimate governing authority originate? One pole holds that the expressed will of the general population is the only valid source of political legitimacy, favoring direct participation, referenda, recall elections, and skepticism of credentialed elites. The other holds that effective governance requires delegation to trained specialists, established institutions, and technocratic bodies insulated from popular pressure. This captures the populism–technocracy divide that has become one of the most important political cleavages globally, and it is orthogonal to the democratic–autocratic distinction — a populist and a technocrat can both be committed democrats.

**Axis 5 — Liberty ↔ Security**

The Hobbesian tradeoff. One pole prioritizes strict limits on state power: inviolable privacy, freedom of expression, constraints on surveillance and policing, even at the cost of increased vulnerability to crime or external threat. The other prioritizes the state's duty to protect: robust law enforcement, surveillance capabilities, decisive intervention against threats, even at the cost of individual privacy and autonomy.

**Axis 6 — Source of Legitimacy: Electoral ↔ Performance-Based**

This axis is critical for cross-cultural validity and is absent from every existing popular quiz. It asks whether a government's right to govern derives primarily from *how it came to power* (free elections, consent of the governed, procedural legitimacy) or from *what it delivers* (economic growth, social stability, national prestige, effective services). In Western democratic theory, these are often assumed to be the same thing. They are not. Developmental states in East Asia, monarchies in the Gulf, and one-party states in various regions derive legitimacy primarily from performance and output. Capturing this dimension is essential for any instrument that claims to be country-agnostic.

### Domain C: Society and Identity

**Axis 7 — Progressive Change ↔ Continuity and Tradition**

The cultural dimension's broadest expression. One pole holds that inherited social structures, norms, and institutions should be continuously scrutinized and reformed to maximize inclusion, individual autonomy, and adaptation to new knowledge. The other holds that these structures represent accumulated practical wisdom — tested by time, essential for social cohesion — and should be preserved, reformed cautiously if at all.

This is the second of the two empirically robust "super-dimensions" in political science, appearing as GAL-TAN in CHES, Traditional/Secular-Rational in the World Values Survey, and mapping onto the Authority/Sanctity/Loyalty moral foundations.

**Axis 8 — Pluralism ↔ Cohesion**

When a society contains multiple cultural, ethnic, linguistic, or religious groups, should the state actively protect and accommodate that diversity, or should it cultivate a unified national identity that all residents are expected to adopt? One pole views diversity as a source of resilience and innovation worth institutional protection. The other views it as a centrifugal force that, without active assimilation into shared norms, leads to fragmentation and conflict.

**Axis 9 — Constructivism ↔ Essentialism**

A deeper ontological axis beneath the progressive–traditional divide. One pole holds that human behavior, social hierarchies, and identity categories are primarily constructed by culture and institutions, and therefore can be reconstructed through deliberate policy. The other holds that there are durable features of human nature — whether biological, evolutionary, or spiritual in origin — that governance must accommodate rather than attempt to override. This axis determines not just what someone wants to change, but whether they believe change of that kind is *possible*.

### Domain D: The State in the World

**Axis 10 — Internationalism ↔ Sovereignty**

Does the state's primary obligation extend beyond its borders? One pole favors deep international cooperation, binding multilateral agreements, open borders, and the pooling of sovereignty to address transnational challenges. The other holds that the state's duty is to its own citizens first, that sovereignty is non-negotiable, and that supranational institutions represent threats to self-determination.

**Axis 11 — Interventionism ↔ Non-Interventionism**

Independent of how internationally engaged a state is, this axis asks whether it should project hard power abroad. One pole views military strength and the willingness to use it as essential tools of statecraft — for deterrence, for protecting allies, for maintaining favorable order. The other views military force as destructive, economically wasteful, and morally corrosive, favoring diplomacy, demilitarization, and strict non-intervention even in the face of foreign atrocities.

**Axis 12 — Innovation Imperative ↔ Precautionary Governance**

As emerging technologies (AI, genetic engineering, autonomous systems, surveillance infrastructure) reshape what governance *can* do, how should the state relate to technological risk? One pole holds that the benefits of rapid innovation outweigh hypothetical risks, that regulation should be light and reactive, and that technological progress is humanity's best tool for solving its problems. The other holds that powerful technologies with poorly understood consequences should be restricted until proven safe, that the burden of proof falls on innovators, and that some capabilities should be foreclosed entirely regardless of their potential benefits.

### Dimensions Considered and Excluded

Several dimensions from existing quizzes and from the Gemini research were evaluated and intentionally excluded or merged:

**Rehabilitative vs. Punitive Justice** — After analysis, this loads primarily onto the Progressive–Traditional axis (Axis 7) and the Constructivism–Essentialism axis (Axis 9). In cross-cultural factor analyses, justice attitudes do not form a reliably independent factor. Including it as a standalone axis would add questions without adding discriminant validity.

**Transhumanism vs. Bioconservatism** — A genuinely novel dimension, but too narrow and speculative to justify 5–6 questions in a general-purpose instrument. It correlates heavily with the Innovation–Precautionary axis (Axis 12) and with Progressive–Traditional (Axis 7). It could be included in a future "Emerging Frontiers" expansion module.

**Militarism as a separate axis from Interventionism** — Some frameworks separate the desire for military *capability* from the willingness to *use* it. In practice, these are highly correlated. The Interventionism axis captures both.

## 3. Question Design and Elicitation Methods

The instrument uses three distinct question modalities, sequenced to move from abstract principles to concrete governance decisions. Total length: **72 core questions** (6 per axis), plus one budget allocation exercise. Estimated completion time: **18–24 minutes**.

### Modality 1: Governance Dilemmas (Forced-Choice Tradeoffs)

**36 questions — 3 per axis**

Each question presents two statements representing opposing poles of a single axis. The respondent must choose which statement comes closer to their view. There is no neutral option, no "both," and no scale. This is the instrument's primary defense against acquiescence bias.

The statements are designed to be roughly equally defensible. Neither option is a strawman. Both are written in their strongest form.

**Example items:**

*Axis 1 — Collective Provision ↔ Market Allocation:*
> When individuals face hardships they cannot overcome alone, organized society should guarantee they receive the support they need — even if this requires significant redistribution of resources.
>
> **OR**
>
> Individuals and their families bear primary responsibility for their own welfare — a society that guarantees outcomes rather than opportunities undermines the initiative that produces shared prosperity.

*Axis 5 — Liberty ↔ Security:*
> It is better that some genuinely dangerous individuals evade detection than that the state gains the power to monitor the private communications of ordinary people.
>
> **OR**
>
> The state has a duty to use every available tool — including broad surveillance — to protect its people from catastrophic threats, even if this reduces individual privacy.

*Axis 6 — Electoral Legitimacy ↔ Performance Legitimacy:*
> A government that came to power through free and fair elections is legitimate even if it governs poorly — the process matters more than the outcome.
>
> **OR**
>
> A government that delivers sustained prosperity, stability, and effective services is legitimate even if it was not chosen through competitive elections — results matter more than process.

*Axis 12 — Innovation ↔ Precaution:*
> When a powerful new technology could produce enormous benefits but carries uncertain risks, society should move forward and correct problems as they emerge — delay itself has costs.
>
> **OR**
>
> When a powerful new technology carries uncertain risks, it should not be deployed at scale until those risks are well understood — some harms, once done, cannot be undone.

**Design notes for forced-choice items:**
- Each axis has 3 forced-choice items, worded at different levels of abstraction (one philosophical, one institutional, one concrete-scenario)
- Item order is randomized within and across axes
- Both poles are written in first-person-inclusive language ("society should..." not "you should...") to reduce social desirability bias
- No item references a specific country, political figure, party, policy name, or institution
- The portrait method (adapted from Schwartz's validated cross-cultural technique) is used for culturally sensitive items: "Imagine a person who believes [X]. How similar is this person to you?" This avoids asking respondents to directly endorse controversial positions

### Modality 2: Calibrated Scales (Construct-Specific 5-Point)

**36 questions — 3 per axis**

For each axis, three additional items use a 5-point scale with construct-specific anchors (not agree/disagree). This captures *intensity* that the forced-choice format cannot.

**Example:**

*Axis 3 — Distributed ↔ Centralized Governance:*
> How much authority should local communities have to set their own laws, even when those laws conflict with national standards?

| Almost none — national standards should be uniform | Limited — on minor matters only | Moderate — on most domestic issues | Extensive — on nearly all issues except defense and currency | Near-total — local communities should be essentially self-governing |
|---|---|---|---|---|

*Axis 10 — Internationalism ↔ Sovereignty:*
> When an international agreement would benefit the world as a whole but impose costs on your nation specifically, how readily should your government agree to it?

| Almost always — global welfare takes priority | Usually — unless the costs are severe | It depends — costs and benefits must be roughly balanced | Rarely — national interest should come first in most cases | Almost never — sovereignty should not be compromised for external agreements |

**Design notes for scaled items:**
- Anchors describe the *construct* (how much authority, how readily), never "agree/disagree"
- Items are balanced: for each axis, roughly half are keyed toward each pole, preventing directional bias
- The 5 points represent a genuine continuum of governance positions, not intensity of agreement with a premise
- Items avoid double-barreling (testing only one axis per item)

### Modality 3: The Chancellor's Budget (Resource Allocation Simulation)

**1 interactive exercise — approximately 5 minutes**

The respondent assumes the role of head of state of a generic, medium-sized nation and must allocate **100 units of national budget** across 10 ministries. Each ministry has a minimum viable allocation of 5 units (below which the simulation warns of state failure in that domain), consuming 50 units. The remaining 50 units are discretionary.

**The 10 Ministries:**

| Ministry | Maps to Axes |
|---|---|
| Social Welfare & Public Health | 1 (Collective Provision) |
| Economic Development & Trade | 1 (Market Allocation), 2 (Growth) |
| Ecological Transition & Conservation | 2 (Ecological Limits) |
| Defense & Military | 11 (Interventionism) |
| Domestic Security & Law Enforcement | 5 (Security) |
| Education & Research | 4 (Institutional Authority), 12 (Innovation) |
| Cultural Heritage & National Identity | 7 (Tradition), 8 (Cohesion) |
| Infrastructure & Technology | 12 (Innovation), 2 (Growth) |
| Foreign Affairs & International Cooperation | 10 (Internationalism) |
| Civil Liberties & Judicial Independence | 5 (Liberty), 6 (Electoral Legitimacy) |

**What the budget simulation captures that other modalities cannot:**

The simulation enforces *scarcity*. A respondent who rated "Strongly Agree" on both environmental protection and economic growth in a Likert quiz is forced here to decide which one actually gets funded when both can't be. The allocation pattern reveals a priority hierarchy among values — the respondent's *ordering* of their commitments, not just the commitments themselves.

The simulation also enables **contradiction detection**. If a respondent's forced-choice answers strongly favor one pole but their budget allocation contradicts that preference, the scoring algorithm detects the inconsistency and weights the revealed-preference data (the budget) more heavily than the stated-preference data (the forced-choice). This is flagged in results as a "tension" rather than an error — it's genuinely informative to know where someone's principles and practical priorities diverge.

**Design notes for the budget simulation:**
- The ministries are described in functional, country-agnostic terms (no country-specific names or structures)
- The interface shows real-time consequences: reducing one ministry below threshold triggers a brief, neutral description of what degrades (e.g., "Military readiness falls below deterrence threshold" — not "Your country is invaded")
- The exercise is introduced as exploratory, not evaluative — there are no right answers, only tradeoffs
- Allocations are normalized and mapped onto axis scores as a weighted supplement to the question-based scores

## 4. Scoring Architecture

### Primary Scoring: Axis Coordinates

Each axis produces a score from -1.0 (strong first pole) to +1.0 (strong second pole), with 0.0 representing genuine ambivalence. The score is a weighted composite:

- **Forced-choice items** (Modality 1): 40% weight — establishes directional preference
- **Scaled items** (Modality 2): 35% weight — establishes intensity
- **Budget allocation** (Modality 3): 25% weight — reveals operational priorities

The budget simulation provides axis scores only for the axes it directly maps to. For axes with no budget mapping (Axis 9: Constructivism–Essentialism, for instance), the score is derived entirely from Modalities 1 and 2.

### Contradiction Index

For each axis, the algorithm computes a consistency score between the three modalities. High consistency (all three modalities agree on direction and intensity) produces a high-confidence score. Low consistency (e.g., strongly liberty-favoring in forced-choice but heavily funding security in the budget) produces the same axis score but with a lower confidence indicator and a flagged "internal tension." This is displayed to the user as a feature, not a bug — the instrument acknowledges that holding conflicting commitments is normal and informative.

### Hierarchical Dimension Reduction

For simplified visualization, the 12 axes are organized into a 2-level hierarchy:

**Super-Dimension 1: Economic** (Axes 1, 2)
A composite capturing overall economic orientation from state-directed egalitarianism to market-driven growth.

**Super-Dimension 2: Cultural-Authority** (Axes 7, 8, 9)
A composite capturing overall orientation from progressive-pluralist-constructivist to traditional-cohesive-essentialist.

**Secondary Dimensions:**
- Governance Structure (Axes 3, 4, 6) — how power is organized
- State Posture (Axes 5, 10, 11) — how the state relates to threats and the world
- Frontier Orientation (Axis 12) — relationship to emerging risk

This allows the instrument to produce both a two-dimensional "compass" plot (Economic × Cultural-Authority, the two validated super-dimensions) and a full 12-axis profile.

## 5. Results Presentation

Results are presented in four layers, from simplest to most detailed.

### Layer 1: The Compass Plot

A two-dimensional scatter plot using the two super-dimensions. This is the most shareable, most intuitive output — a single dot on a plane. It answers the question "roughly, where do I sit?" and is directly comparable to the Political Compass format that most users are already familiar with, but computed from a much richer data set.

### Layer 2: The Radar Chart (Full Profile)

A 12-spoke radar/spider chart showing the respondent's score on every axis. This is the instrument's signature visualization — it produces a unique geometric "fingerprint" for each respondent. The shape communicates instantly: a shape that spikes toward all the "first poles" (collective, distributed, liberty, progressive, internationalist, precautionary) looks qualitatively different from one that spikes toward all the "second poles."

The radar chart supports overlays: the respondent's shape can be compared to named archetypes, to aggregate national profiles, or to other users who share results.

### Layer 3: The Archetype

Using Latent Class Analysis on the full 12-axis score vector, the respondent is assigned to one of approximately 12–16 named archetypes derived from cluster analysis of a large reference population. Archetypes are described in narrative terms — not just a label, but a 2-3 paragraph description of the worldview, its internal logic, its characteristic tensions, and the governance traditions it most closely resembles.

Example archetypes (illustrative — final archetypes would emerge from empirical clustering):

- **The Civic Institutionalist** — Favors democratic process, distributed governance, strong civil liberties, moderate market economics, and cautious internationalism. Trusts institutional design over both populist energy and technocratic authority. Characteristic tension: commitment to democratic process can conflict with desire for effective governance when popular will produces poor policy.

- **The Developmental Modernizer** — Favors centralized governance, performance-based legitimacy, rapid innovation, growth-oriented economics, and strong state capacity. Prioritizes results over process. Characteristic tension: belief in institutional authority can conflict with commitment to individual liberty when the state's modernization agenda meets cultural resistance.

- **The Communitarian Steward** — Favors ecological limits, distributed governance, cultural cohesion, traditional values, and non-interventionism. Seeks small-scale, rooted, sustainable communities. Characteristic tension: commitment to local autonomy can conflict with desire for cultural cohesion when different communities develop in divergent directions.

- **The Cosmopolitan Technologist** — Favors internationalism, innovation, progressive social values, market economics, and technocratic governance. Believes global coordination and technological progress can solve most problems. Characteristic tension: faith in expert governance can conflict with commitment to progressive values when expertise produces recommendations that feel culturally regressive.

### Layer 4: The Detailed Report

A full breakdown showing:
- Each axis score with confidence interval
- The specific items that contributed most to each score
- Flagged internal tensions (modality contradictions)
- Comparison to reference populations (if available)
- The moral foundations profile implied by the axis scores (mapping from governance preferences back to the psychological substrate)

## 6. Cross-Cultural Validity Framework

This is where the instrument departs most significantly from every existing popular quiz. Cross-cultural validity is not an afterthought — it is a structural design constraint applied to every question, every axis definition, and every result interpretation.

### Question-Level Safeguards

**Abstract governance framing.** No question references a specific country, leader, party, policy by name, constitutional provision, or institutional structure. "Universal healthcare" becomes "Should organized society guarantee that all people receive medical treatment regardless of their ability to pay?" "Second Amendment" becomes "Should individuals have the right to possess weapons for personal defense even if this increases overall rates of violence?"

**The portrait method for sensitive items.** Adapted from Schwartz's Portrait Values Questionnaire — validated across 49 cultural groups in the European Social Survey — sensitive items are framed as descriptions of a hypothetical person: "This person believes that the government's most important job is to keep people safe, even if it means limiting some personal freedoms. How much is this person like you?" This format reduces social desirability bias and avoids requiring direct endorsement of positions that may carry stigma in specific cultures.

**Avoidance of culturally loaded terms.** The words "liberal," "conservative," "left," "right," "progressive," and "traditional" never appear in question text — these carry opposite meanings across cultures. "Free speech" is decomposed into its underlying tension (expression vs. social harm). "Democracy" is decomposed into its component features (elections, rule of law, accountability, participation) rather than used as a monolithic concept.

### Instrument-Level Validation Protocol

Before deployment, the instrument undergoes:

1. **Exploratory Factor Analysis** (N ≥ 500 in development sample) — confirming that the 12 axes emerge as distinct factors from the item pool
2. **Confirmatory Factor Analysis** (separate validation sample, N ≥ 500) — confirming the factor structure holds
3. **Multi-Group Measurement Invariance Testing** across at minimum 5 culturally distinct populations (testing configural, metric, and scalar invariance) — confirming that the same constructs are being measured across cultures
4. **Differential Item Functioning (DIF) analysis** — identifying and revising items that function differently across demographic groups
5. **Test-Retest Reliability** (2-4 week interval, N ≥ 200) — confirming score stability
6. **Convergent Validity** against voting behavior, party identification, and established instruments (World Values Survey items, Schwartz PVQ, Moral Foundations Questionnaire)

No popular political quiz has undergone any of these procedures. Meeting these standards would be the single most important differentiator of this instrument.

### Modular Architecture

The 72-question core instrument is designed for universal deployment. Optional regional modules (10–15 questions each) can be appended for specific contexts, following the European Social Survey and Afrobarometer model. Regional modules might address:

- Specific institutional contexts (e.g., attitudes toward the EU, ASEAN, African Union)
- Region-specific governance tensions (e.g., caste-based reservation policy in South Asia, land reform in Southern Africa, language policy in multilingual states)
- Candidate/party matching (ISideWith-style) for specific elections

The core results are always comparable across cultures. Regional modules add context without compromising comparability.

## 7. Technical Architecture (Brief)

### Platform
Web-based responsive application. The budget simulation requires an interactive UI; the forced-choice and scaled items are standard form elements. No app installation required.

### Data
All responses stored anonymously with optional demographic metadata (country, age range, education level, urbanization) for aggregate analysis. Individual results are never shared without explicit user consent.

### Scoring
Real-time scoring in the client for immediate results. Archetype assignment requires server-side computation against the reference population clustering model.

### Open Source
The question bank, scoring algorithm, axis definitions, and validation data should be fully open and auditable. This is a direct response to the Political Compass's opaque, proprietary scoring — and a prerequisite for scientific credibility.

## 8. What This Instrument Does Not Do

**It does not tell you who to vote for.** This is a values and governance-principles instrument, not a candidate matcher. The optional regional modules could include candidate matching, but the core instrument is deliberately election-agnostic.

**It does not claim false precision.** Every score includes a confidence indicator. The archetype is presented as "closest match," not "this is who you are." The compass plot is presented as a simplification, with a clear invitation to explore the full radar profile.

**It does not assume democracy is the correct answer.** Axis 6 (Electoral ↔ Performance Legitimacy) explicitly includes non-democratic governance as a coherent position. Axis 3 (Distributed ↔ Centralized) separates the structure of power from its origin. This is essential for an instrument that claims to be country-agnostic rather than democracy-advocacy disguised as measurement.

**It does not pathologize any position.** Every pole of every axis is described in its strongest, most defensible form. Neither "Security" nor "Liberty" is framed as the obvious correct answer. Neither "Tradition" nor "Progress" is treated as inherently superior. The instrument measures where you stand, not whether you're right.

## Appendix B: Sample Questions by Axis (Illustrative, Not Final)

### Axis 1 — Collective Provision ↔ Market Allocation

**Forced-Choice:**
> A society where no one falls below a dignified standard of living — even if this limits what the most productive members can accumulate — is preferable to one where anyone can rise to extraordinary wealth but some inevitably fall into severe deprivation.
>
> **OR**
>
> A society where individual effort and talent determine outcomes — even if this means some people live in hardship — is preferable to one where guaranteed outcomes reduce the incentive to innovate and strive.

**Scaled:**
> How large a role should the state play in determining how a society's wealth is distributed?

| Dominant — the state should directly control most economic activity | Major — through heavy taxation and comprehensive public services | Moderate — a market economy with meaningful regulation and a safety net | Limited — light regulation with targeted assistance only for extreme need | Minimal — the state should have almost no role in economic distribution |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 6 — Electoral Process ↔ Performance Outcomes

**Forced-Choice (Portrait Method):**
> Person A believes that the most important thing about a government is that the people chose it freely — even if it sometimes makes poor decisions, the right to choose is sacred.
>
> Person B believes that the most important thing about a government is that it delivers good results — prosperity, safety, effective services — regardless of how its leaders were selected.
>
> Which person's view is closer to your own?

**Scaled:**
> If a government that was not freely elected consistently delivered excellent public services, economic growth, and personal safety for its citizens, how legitimate would you consider that government?

| Fully legitimate — results are what matter | Mostly legitimate — but elections would still be preferable | Somewhat legitimate — competence matters, but so does consent | Mostly illegitimate — good results don't justify unelected power | Completely illegitimate — only governments chosen by the people have the right to govern |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 12 — Precautionary ↔ Innovation-First

**Forced-Choice:**
> A breakthrough technology that could cure a major disease but carries a small risk of catastrophic unintended consequences should be deployed as quickly as possible — the certain suffering happening now outweighs uncertain future risks.
>
> **OR**
>
> A breakthrough technology that carries any risk of catastrophic unintended consequences should not be deployed until those risks are thoroughly understood — irreversible harms are categorically different from harms we already know how to address.

**Scaled:**
> When a government must decide whether to permit a powerful but poorly understood new technology, where should the burden of proof fall?

| Entirely on those who want to restrict it — innovation should be the default | Mostly on restrictors — with reasonable safety standards | Balanced — both sides should make their case | Mostly on innovators — they must demonstrate safety first | Entirely on innovators — nothing should be deployed until proven safe |
|---|---|---|---|---|
