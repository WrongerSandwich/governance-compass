# The Governance Compass — Complete Question Bank

## Reference Guide

This document contains the full item bank for The Governance Compass political belief system assessment. It is intended as a specification for implementation, not as a user-facing document.

### Structure

- **12 axes**, each with **6 items**: 3 forced-choice dilemmas + 3 construct-specific scaled items
- **72 total scored questions** across the 12 axes
- **1 budget allocation exercise** (The Chancellor's Budget)
- Estimated completion time: 18–24 minutes

### Question Format Key

**Forced-Choice (FC):** Two statements, respondent picks the one closer to their view. No neutral option. Scored as -1 (Statement A) or +1 (Statement B). Each FC item is tagged with an abstraction level:
- **(P)** = Philosophical/principled — tests the abstract value
- **(I)** = Institutional/structural — tests how it manifests in governance design
- **(S)** = Scenario/applied — tests the value under realistic pressure

**Scaled (SC):** A question with a 5-point construct-specific response scale. Scored from -2 (strong Pole A) to +2 (strong Pole B), with 0 as the genuine midpoint. Anchors are specific to each question — never generic "agree/disagree."

**Portrait (PT):** A variant of forced-choice where two hypothetical people are described and the respondent identifies which is more like them. Used for culturally sensitive items. Scored identically to FC.

### Axis Orientation Convention

Throughout this document, **Pole A** is always listed first and scores negative. **Pole B** is listed second and scores positive. This is an arbitrary convention for scoring purposes and implies no value judgment — neither pole is "better."

## DOMAIN A: ECONOMIC ORGANIZATION

---

### Axis 1 — Collective Provision (A) ↔ Market Allocation (B)

*What role should organized society play in determining economic outcomes and ensuring material welfare?*

**FC-1.1 (P)**
> **A:** A just society guarantees that no person falls below a dignified standard of living — even if this requires substantial redistribution from those who have more to those who have less.
>
> **B:** A just society protects each person's right to keep what they earn and exchange freely — even if this means some people will have far more than others and some will struggle.

**FC-1.2 (I)**
> **A:** The state should directly provide essential services — healthcare, housing, education — as universal public goods funded through taxation, because markets will always leave some people behind.
>
> **B:** Essential services are delivered more effectively when individuals choose among competing private providers — the state's role should be limited to helping those who truly cannot help themselves.

**FC-1.3 (S)**
> **A:** When a major industry fails and thousands face unemployment, the state should intervene — nationalizing the industry if necessary — to protect workers and communities from devastation.
>
> **B:** When a major industry fails, the state should allow it to fail — propping up unviable enterprises with public funds delays necessary adaptation and wastes resources that could be better used elsewhere.

**SC-1.1**
> How large a role should the state play in determining how a society's wealth is distributed?

| Dominant — the state should directly control most economic activity | Major — heavy taxation and comprehensive public services | Moderate — a market economy with meaningful regulation and a safety net | Limited — light regulation with targeted help only for extreme need | Minimal — the state should have almost no role in economic distribution |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-1.2**
> When private companies become so large that they dominate an entire sector of the economy, how should the state respond?

| Break them up and transfer key functions to public ownership | Break them up and heavily regulate successors | Regulate them firmly but allow them to continue operating | Monitor them but intervene only if consumers are clearly harmed | Leave them alone — dominance earned through competition is legitimate |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-1.3**
> When the state collects taxes, what principle should guide how much each person pays?

| Those with more wealth should pay a sharply increasing share — the gap between rich and poor is itself a harm to address | Those with more should pay proportionally more — a graduated approach balancing fairness with incentive | Everyone should pay the same percentage of income — equal proportional treatment is fairest | Everyone should pay a modest flat contribution toward essential services only | Taxation should be as close to zero as possible — people should keep what they earn |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 2 — Ecological Limits (A) ↔ Growth Imperative (B)

*Is continuous economic expansion compatible with a finite planet, and should governance prioritize sustainability over prosperity?*

**FC-2.1 (P)**
> **A:** There are hard physical limits to how much humanity can produce and consume — a responsible society must learn to thrive within ecological boundaries, even if this means accepting less material wealth.
>
> **B:** Human ingenuity has always found ways to do more with less — the path to sustainability runs through innovation and growth, not through accepting scarcity and reduced living standards.

**FC-2.2 (I)**
> **A:** Economic policy should be evaluated primarily by whether it maintains or restores ecological health — GDP growth is a misleading measure of genuine societal progress.
>
> **B:** Economic growth remains the most reliable way to fund environmental protection, lift people out of poverty, and develop the clean technologies the planet needs — shrinking the economy would make every problem harder to solve.

**FC-2.3 (S)**
> **A:** If a nation's primary export industry is profitable but ecologically destructive, the government should phase it out over a defined timeline — even if no replacement industry is yet available — because continued environmental damage will ultimately cost more than the economic disruption.
>
> **B:** If a nation's primary export industry is profitable but ecologically destructive, the government should invest heavily in making that industry cleaner rather than shutting it down — abrupt economic disruption harms the very people environmentalism claims to protect.

**SC-2.1**
> If economic growth and environmental protection come into direct conflict, which should a government prioritize?

| Environment, always — ecological health is a precondition for everything else | Environment in most cases — with exceptions only for preventing severe human hardship | Balance both — neither should consistently override the other | Growth in most cases — prosperity provides the resources to address environmental problems later | Growth, always — economic development is the most urgent priority for human welfare |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-2.2**
> How strictly should the state limit industrial activity that damages the natural environment?

| Strictly prohibit any activity with significant ecological impact, even if economically costly | Impose firm limits with strong enforcement and penalties | Regulate with meaningful standards but balance against economic needs | Set voluntary guidelines and incentivize compliance rather than mandating it | Minimal intervention — environmental issues are best addressed through innovation and market demand |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-2.3**
> Should a nation's measure of success include ecological health alongside economic output?

| Ecological health should replace economic output as the primary measure of national success | Ecological measures should be weighted equally with economic ones | Both matter, but economic output remains the more practical benchmark | Ecological metrics are useful supplements but shouldn't drive policy | National success is best measured by economic output — ecology is a separate concern |
|---|---|---|---|---|

## DOMAIN B: POWER AND AUTHORITY

---

### Axis 3 — Distributed Governance (A) ↔ Centralized Governance (B)

*How should political and administrative power be distributed across geographic and institutional levels?*

**FC-3.1 (P)**
> **A:** The people closest to a problem are best positioned to solve it — governance should be as local as possible, with power flowing upward only when absolutely necessary.
>
> **B:** Local governance produces inconsistency, duplication, and inequality between regions — a strong central authority ensures that standards are uniform and resources are allocated where they're most needed.

**FC-3.2 (I)**
> **A:** Different regions within a nation should have the authority to set their own laws on most domestic matters — even if this means neighboring regions have very different rules on the same issues.
>
> **B:** A nation's laws should be uniform across all its territory — allowing regions to diverge creates confusion, inequality, and undermines national identity.

**FC-3.3 (S)**
> **A:** When a national government and a local community disagree about how land should be used — for example, whether to build infrastructure that benefits the nation but disrupts the community — the local community's wishes should prevail.
>
> **B:** When a national government and a local community disagree about land use, the national interest should prevail — individual communities cannot be allowed to block projects that benefit the broader population.

**SC-3.1**
> How much authority should local communities have to set their own laws, even when those laws differ from national standards?

| Near-total — local communities should be essentially self-governing | Extensive — on nearly all issues except defense and currency | Moderate — on most domestic issues with national minimum standards | Limited — on minor local matters only | Almost none — national standards should be uniform everywhere |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-3.2**
> When a policy problem spans multiple regions — such as river pollution or transportation networks — who should have primary authority to address it?

| Local communities should negotiate solutions among themselves | Regional bodies should coordinate, with local input | A mix of national direction and regional implementation | The national government should lead, consulting regions as needed | The national government should decide and implement directly |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-3.3**
> How important is it that all citizens of a nation live under the same basic rules and standards?

| Not very — diversity of local governance is a strength | Somewhat — a few core standards are needed but most rules should vary locally | Moderately — a meaningful common framework with room for local variation | Very — most rules should be consistent, with limited local exceptions | Essential — uniform standards are fundamental to national fairness and cohesion |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 4 — Popular Sovereignty (A) ↔ Institutional Authority (B)

*Should governance be guided primarily by the expressed will of ordinary people, or by the judgment of trained specialists and established institutions?*

**FC-4.1 (P)**
> **A:** The collective wisdom of ordinary people — grounded in lived experience and common sense — is a more trustworthy guide to good governance than the theories of credentialed experts, who are often disconnected from the realities most people face.
>
> **B:** The challenges of modern governance are too complex for popular intuition alone — societies are better served when policy is shaped by people with deep specialized knowledge, even when their conclusions are unpopular.

**FC-4.2 (I)**
> **A:** Major policy decisions should be put directly to the population through referenda and public votes whenever possible — elected representatives and bureaucrats should implement the people's will, not substitute their own judgment.
>
> **B:** Major policy decisions should be made by elected representatives advised by subject-matter experts — direct popular votes on complex technical issues produce worse outcomes than informed deliberation.

**FC-4.3 (S)**
> **A:** When scientific consensus on a policy issue contradicts strongly held public opinion, the government should follow public opinion — in a society that serves its people, the people's values must take precedence over any expert recommendation.
>
> **B:** When scientific consensus on a policy issue contradicts strongly held public opinion, the government should follow the science — governing well sometimes means making decisions that are unpopular because they are correct.

**SC-4.1**
> When experts and the general public disagree about the right course of action, whose judgment should carry more weight in government policy?

| The public's — always. Governance exists to serve the people's expressed wishes | The public's — in most cases, with narrow exceptions for technical emergencies | Both should carry equal weight | The experts' — in most cases, with public input on values and priorities | The experts' — always. Complex governance requires specialized knowledge |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-4.2**
> How much direct control should ordinary citizens have over day-to-day government policy?

| Maximum — citizens should vote directly on major issues regularly | Substantial — through frequent referenda and strong recall mechanisms | Moderate — through elected representatives who are accountable at election time | Limited — citizens choose leaders who then govern using expert advice | Minimal — governance is best handled by qualified professionals with broad authority |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-4.3**
> When a government agency staffed by specialists makes a decision that most citizens oppose, what should happen?

| The decision should be immediately reversed — public opposition invalidates it | The decision should be suspended and put to a public vote | The decision should be reviewed, with public concerns given serious weight | The decision should stand unless there are clear errors — expertise should be trusted | The decision should stand — insulating technical governance from popular pressure produces better outcomes |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 5 — Liberty (A) ↔ Security (B)

*When individual freedom and collective safety conflict, which should the state prioritize?*

**FC-5.1 (P)**
> **A:** It is better that some genuinely dangerous individuals go undetected than that the state acquires the power to monitor the private lives of ordinary people.
>
> **B:** It is better that ordinary people sacrifice some privacy than that genuinely dangerous individuals remain free to cause catastrophic harm.

**FC-5.2 (I)**
> **A:** Constitutional protections for individual rights — speech, privacy, assembly, due process — should be nearly absolute, even during emergencies. A state that suspends rights under pressure will always find new reasons to keep them suspended.
>
> **B:** Constitutional protections must be balanced against the state's duty to protect its people. During genuine crises, temporary restrictions on individual rights are not only acceptable but necessary — a state that cannot act decisively when threatened will not survive to protect anyone's rights.

**FC-5.3 (S)**
> **A:** If intelligence agencies report that broad surveillance of digital communications could prevent rare but devastating attacks, the government should refuse — the certainty of mass privacy violation outweighs the possibility of preventing harm.
>
> **B:** If intelligence agencies report that broad surveillance of digital communications could prevent rare but devastating attacks, the government should authorize it — the duty to prevent catastrophic loss of life outweighs the cost to individual privacy.

**SC-5.1**
> How much power should law enforcement have to investigate and detain individuals suspected of planning serious harm?

| Strictly limited — strong protections for suspects are essential even if some threats go undetected | Somewhat limited — with firm judicial oversight at every stage | Moderate — balanced powers with meaningful but not absolute safeguards | Broad — with general oversight but operational flexibility for authorities | Expansive — public safety requires giving law enforcement wide latitude to act on suspicion |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-5.2**
> How acceptable is it for a government to restrict freedom of expression when speech may incite violence or undermine social stability?

| Never acceptable — expression must be protected regardless of content or consequence | Rarely — only in cases of direct, immediate incitement to specific violence | Sometimes — when there is a clear and demonstrable risk to public safety | Often — maintaining social order justifies meaningful limits on provocative or destabilizing speech | Almost always — the state has a duty to prevent speech that threatens social cohesion |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-5.3**
> In your view, which poses a greater long-term threat to a good society?

| A state with too much power is always the greater danger | A state with too much power is usually the greater danger | Both are roughly equal threats | Unchecked disorder and crime are usually the greater danger | Unchecked disorder and crime are always the greater danger |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 6 — Electoral Process (A) ↔ Performance Outcomes (B)

*Does a government's legitimacy come primarily from how it was chosen, or from what it delivers?*

**PT-6.1 (P)** *(Portrait method)*
> **Person A** believes that a government's right to govern comes from the free choice of the people — a government that was not chosen through fair, competitive elections is never fully legitimate, no matter how well it performs.
>
> **Person B** believes that a government's right to govern comes from results — a government that delivers prosperity, safety, and effective services is legitimate regardless of how its leaders were selected.
>
> *Which person's view is closer to your own?*

**FC-6.2 (I)**
> **A:** A society should always maintain competitive elections, independent courts, and free press — even when these institutions slow decision-making and sometimes produce poor leaders — because the process of choosing is more important than the quality of who is chosen.
>
> **B:** A society should be structured to identify and empower the most capable leaders — even if this means limiting electoral competition — because the quality of governance matters more than the method of selecting governors.

**FC-6.3 (S)**
> **A:** A freely elected government that presides over economic decline, rising crime, and deteriorating public services is still more legitimate than an unelected government that delivers prosperity and safety — because legitimacy is about consent, not outcomes.
>
> **B:** An unelected government that delivers prosperity, safety, and effective services has earned more practical legitimacy than an elected government that has failed to govern well — because people need good governance more than they need to have chosen their governors.

**SC-6.1**
> If a government that was not freely elected consistently delivered excellent public services, economic growth, and personal safety, how legitimate would you consider it?

| Completely illegitimate — only governments chosen by the people have the right to govern | Mostly illegitimate — good results don't justify unelected power | Somewhat legitimate — competence matters, but so does consent | Mostly legitimate — results matter more, though elections would be preferable | Fully legitimate — what a government delivers matters more than how it came to power |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-6.2**
> How important is it that citizens have the power to remove their leaders through elections?

| Absolutely essential — this is the foundation of legitimate governance | Very important — the primary mechanism, though not the only one | Important — one of several factors that make governance legitimate | Somewhat important — but competent governance matters more | Not essential — there are other valid ways to ensure accountability |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-6.3**
> When a government faces a long-term challenge that requires unpopular decisions over many years, what approach is most appropriate?

| Leaders should always follow public opinion, even if experts believe the public is wrong — that is what consent of the governed means | Leaders should explain their reasoning and persuade the public, proceeding only with public support | Leaders should balance expert advice with public opinion, accepting some compromise on both sides | Leaders should make the expert-recommended decision and accept the electoral consequences | Leaders should be insulated from short-term public opinion so they can govern for the long term |
|---|---|---|---|---|

## DOMAIN C: SOCIETY AND IDENTITY

---

### Axis 7 — Progressive Change (A) ↔ Continuity and Tradition (B)

*Should a society actively reform its inherited norms and institutions, or preserve them as sources of stability and meaning?*

**FC-7.1 (P)**
> **A:** Inherited customs, hierarchies, and institutions should be continuously re-examined in light of new knowledge and evolving moral understanding — what was once accepted is not automatically worth preserving.
>
> **B:** Inherited customs, hierarchies, and institutions represent the accumulated wisdom of generations who faced real challenges — they should not be discarded simply because they conflict with contemporary fashions in thought.

**FC-7.2 (I)**
> **A:** Laws and social norms should evolve as society's understanding evolves — when evidence shows that an established practice causes unnecessary harm or exclusion, the state has an obligation to change it.
>
> **B:** Laws and social norms should change slowly and cautiously — rapid reform driven by intellectual movements risks destroying functional social structures before their value is understood, causing more harm than it prevents.

**FC-7.3 (S)**
> **A:** When a longstanding cultural practice comes into conflict with principles of individual equality and autonomy, the practice should give way — no tradition justifies the restriction of a person's fundamental rights.
>
> **B:** When a longstanding cultural practice comes into conflict with contemporary notions of equality, society should proceed with great caution — the practice may serve social functions that are not immediately visible, and imposing abstract principles on living communities often backfires.

**SC-7.1**
> How quickly should a society change its social norms and cultural institutions?

| As rapidly as evidence and moral reasoning demand — delay perpetuates harm | At a steady pace — actively pursuing reform while monitoring consequences | Gradually — significant changes should be tested in limited contexts before broad adoption | Slowly — only when problems are severe and alternatives are well-proven | Very reluctantly — the default should be preservation unless the case for change is overwhelming |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-7.2**
> When established religious or cultural institutions hold views that conflict with newer social values, how should the state respond?

| Actively challenge those institutions and restrict practices that conflict with contemporary principles | Withhold public support or recognition from institutions that resist reform | Remain neutral — neither promoting nor restricting the institutions' traditional views | Protect those institutions' right to maintain their traditional positions, even when controversial | Actively support and preserve traditional institutions as vital sources of social cohesion |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-7.3**
> In your view, is the general trajectory of social change over the past century best described as:

| Overwhelmingly positive — a story of expanding freedom, inclusion, and understanding | Mostly positive — with meaningful gains, though not every change has been beneficial | Mixed — some changes have been clearly good, others clearly harmful | Mostly concerning — important things have been lost in the pursuit of novelty | Deeply troubling — foundational values and institutions have been eroded in damaging ways |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 8 — Pluralism (A) ↔ Cohesion (B)

*When a society contains diverse groups, should the state protect that diversity or cultivate a unified identity?*

**FC-8.1 (P)**
> **A:** A society is strengthened by containing many distinct cultural traditions, languages, and ways of life — the state should actively protect and accommodate this diversity rather than pressing everyone toward a single mold.
>
> **B:** A society functions best when its members share a common language, set of civic values, and cultural reference points — the state should cultivate shared identity rather than institutionalizing permanent division along group lines.

**FC-8.2 (I)**
> **A:** The state should provide public services in multiple languages, recognize diverse cultural practices in law, and ensure that all cultural communities can maintain their distinct identities while participating fully in civic life.
>
> **B:** The state should establish a common civic culture and expect all residents to learn the shared language and adopt shared civic norms — public institutions should unite people around what they have in common, not formalize their differences.

**PT-8.3 (S)** *(Portrait method)*
> **Person A** believes that when immigrants arrive in a new country, the receiving society should adapt to include them — making space for their languages, traditions, and cultural practices in schools, workplaces, and public life.
>
> **Person B** believes that when immigrants arrive in a new country, they should make the primary effort to adapt — learning the common language, adopting local civic norms, and integrating into the existing cultural fabric.
>
> *Which person's view is closer to your own?*

**SC-8.1**
> When cultural or religious practices of a minority group conflict with the majority culture's norms, how should the state respond?

| Actively protect the minority practice — cultural diversity is a fundamental right | Accommodate the minority practice in most cases, seeking compromise where needed | Evaluate case by case — no general rule applies | Expect the minority group to adapt in most cases, making exceptions only for core religious obligations | Expect full adaptation to the majority culture's norms — national cohesion requires shared standards |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-8.2**
> Should public schools primarily teach children about the traditions and history of many cultures, or primarily transmit the shared national culture?

| Primarily many cultures — preparing children for diversity is more important than reinforcing a single identity | Mostly many cultures — with some shared national content | A balance of both | Mostly shared national culture — with respectful acknowledgment of diversity | Primarily shared national culture — schools are where a common identity is formed |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-8.3**
> In a society with multiple ethnic or cultural groups, what level of group distinctiveness is healthiest in the long run?

| Strong distinct identities are a permanent source of strength and should be maintained | Distinct identities should be preserved but with strong bridges between groups | Some blending is natural and healthy, though distinct identities will and should persist | A gradual blending into a shared culture, while respecting heritage, produces the most stable society | A unified common identity is essential — strong group distinctions inevitably breed conflict |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 9 — Constructivism (A) ↔ Essentialism (B)

*Are human behavior and social hierarchies primarily shaped by culture and institutions (and therefore changeable), or by durable features of human nature (and therefore to be accommodated)?*

**FC-9.1 (P)**
> **A:** Most of what we consider "human nature" — including differences in behavior between groups — is shaped by culture, upbringing, and social structures. Change the structures, and you change the outcomes.
>
> **B:** Human beings have a durable nature shaped by biology and deep history. Social structures that ignore this nature — however well-intentioned — will fail, because they are fighting against something more fundamental than policy.

**FC-9.2 (I)**
> **A:** When certain groups are persistently underrepresented in positions of power or prosperity, the most likely explanation is structural barriers that policy should actively dismantle — the goal of governance should be to equalize outcomes.
>
> **B:** When certain groups achieve different outcomes, this may reflect genuine differences in preferences, aptitudes, or cultural priorities rather than structural barriers — the goal of governance should be to equalize access and opportunity, then accept the outcomes that emerge.

**FC-9.3 (S)**
> **A:** A well-designed education system can dramatically reshape society within a generation — if children are raised in the right environment with the right values, most social problems can be solved.
>
> **B:** Education can improve individual lives, but it cannot fundamentally reshape human nature or eliminate all social problems — expecting schools to engineer a new kind of person places an impossible burden on them and ignores deeper realities.

**SC-9.1**
> How much can deliberate policy change the fundamental patterns of human social behavior?

| Almost entirely — social structures determine behavior, and better structures produce better behavior | Substantially — though change takes time and sustained effort | Moderately — policy can shift behavior within limits set by human nature | Somewhat — but human nature constrains what policy can realistically achieve | Very little — human nature is relatively fixed, and policy should work with it rather than against it |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-9.2**
> When a society observes persistent differences in outcomes between groups, what is the most productive response?

| Redesign institutions until outcomes are equal — persistent differences prove the system is failing | Actively reform institutions to remove barriers while tracking outcomes as a measure of progress | Ensure equal access and opportunity, then accept that outcomes may vary for many reasons | Focus on protecting individual rights and freedoms rather than measuring group outcomes | Accept that different outcomes are a natural feature of diverse societies — forced equalization creates new problems |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-9.3**
> To what extent are social hierarchies an inevitable feature of human societies?

| Not at all — hierarchies are entirely constructed and can be entirely eliminated through better design | Mostly not — most current hierarchies are unjust and can be dismantled, though some coordination structures are needed | Partially — some hierarchy may be natural, but much of it is socially constructed and changeable | Mostly — hierarchies emerge naturally in all human groups, though their specific form can be made more humane | Entirely — social hierarchy is a fundamental feature of human nature that governance must accommodate |
|---|---|---|---|---|

## DOMAIN D: THE STATE IN THE WORLD

---

### Axis 10 — Internationalism (A) ↔ Sovereignty (B)

*Should the state participate deeply in international cooperation, or prioritize its own independence and self-determination?*

**FC-10.1 (P)**
> **A:** The most serious challenges facing humanity — climate change, pandemics, economic instability — cannot be solved by any single nation. States must be willing to pool sovereignty and accept binding international agreements, even when those agreements impose costs.
>
> **B:** A nation's first duty is to its own citizens, and binding international agreements inevitably transfer power to distant institutions that are not accountable to the people they affect. Cooperation is valuable, but sovereignty must not be compromised.

**FC-10.2 (I)**
> **A:** International courts, trade organizations, and cooperative bodies should have real authority to enforce agreements and resolve disputes between nations — without enforcement mechanisms, international cooperation is meaningless.
>
> **B:** International bodies should serve as forums for negotiation and coordination, but should never have binding authority over sovereign nations — enforcement power that supersedes national law is a threat to self-determination.

**FC-10.3 (S)**
> **A:** If an international climate agreement requires your nation to make economic sacrifices that other nations are not required to make — because your nation historically contributed more to the problem — your government should accept those terms as a matter of global responsibility.
>
> **B:** If an international climate agreement imposes disproportionate costs on your nation, your government should reject those terms and negotiate for equal treatment — accepting unequal burdens sets a precedent that undermines national interests.

**SC-10.1**
> How willing should a nation be to accept constraints on its own policies in exchange for the benefits of international cooperation?

| Very willing — global coordination requires real compromise from every nation | Mostly willing — with protections against agreements that cause severe domestic harm | Selectively willing — depending on whether the specific agreement serves the national interest | Reluctant — only for the most critical global emergencies | Very reluctant — sovereignty should be compromised only in the most extreme circumstances |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-10.2**
> How open should a nation's borders be to the movement of people?

| Fully open — freedom of movement is a fundamental human right | Broadly open — with basic screening but a presumption of entry | Selectively open — managed immigration based on national needs and humanitarian obligations | Restricted — limited immigration with priority for economic contribution and cultural compatibility | Highly restricted — a nation's borders define its identity and should be firmly controlled |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-10.3**
> When a wealthier nation and a poorer nation negotiate a trade agreement, what principle should guide the terms?

| The wealthier nation should make deliberate concessions to reduce global inequality | Terms should moderately favor the poorer nation to promote development | Terms should be mutually beneficial and equally binding | Each nation should pursue its own best deal — negotiations are inherently competitive | The wealthier nation should use its leverage to secure the best possible terms for its own citizens |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 11 — Non-Interventionism (A) ↔ Interventionism (B)

*Should the state project military force abroad, or commit to strict non-intervention?*

**FC-11.1 (P)**
> **A:** Military force — even when used with good intentions — almost always causes more suffering than it prevents. The history of foreign intervention is overwhelmingly a history of unintended consequences, civilian casualties, and prolonged instability.
>
> **B:** Military strength and the credible willingness to use it are essential to maintaining peace and deterring aggression. A world where aggressive states face no consequences is more dangerous than one where capable nations enforce a baseline of order.

**FC-11.2 (I)**
> **A:** A nation should maintain only the military capacity needed for self-defense and should never deploy forces beyond its own borders — foreign conflicts are not its responsibility.
>
> **B:** A capable nation has both the right and the obligation to maintain military forces that can be projected abroad — to defend allies, protect shipping routes, deter aggression, and uphold international norms.

**FC-11.3 (S)**
> **A:** When a foreign government is committing atrocities against its own civilians, other nations should respond through diplomacy, sanctions, and humanitarian aid — military intervention, even to stop atrocities, violates sovereignty and historically creates more problems than it solves.
>
> **B:** When a foreign government is committing atrocities against its own civilians, capable nations have a moral obligation to intervene militarily if diplomacy fails — sovereignty does not include the right to massacre your own people.

**SC-11.1**
> How much should a nation spend on its military relative to other priorities?

| As little as possible — only enough for minimal border defense | Modestly — enough for credible self-defense but no power projection | Moderately — sufficient for self-defense and limited alliance commitments | Substantially — enough to project force regionally and support allies | As much as needed — military superiority is essential to national security and global stability |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-11.2**
> Under what circumstances is it acceptable for a nation to use military force beyond its own borders?

| Never — military force should be used only in direct self-defense | Only in response to a direct, imminent attack on the nation or its closest allies | When authorized by a legitimate international body to stop an ongoing humanitarian catastrophe | When national strategic interests are seriously threatened, even without imminent attack | Whenever the nation's leaders determine it serves the national interest — military force is a normal tool of statecraft |
|---|---|---|---|---|

**SC-11.3**
> When another nation is building military capability that could threaten your nation in the future — but has not yet acted aggressively — what is the appropriate response?

| Pursue diplomacy and disarmament — military buildups are often responses to perceived threats on the other side | Strengthen alliances and diplomatic pressure while maintaining defensive readiness | Match their buildup proportionally to maintain deterrence | Build a decisive military advantage to ensure they never act on their capability | Consider preemptive action to neutralize the threat before it matures |
|---|---|---|---|---|

### Axis 12 — Precautionary (A) ↔ Innovation-First (B)

*When powerful new technologies carry uncertain risks, should the state restrict them until proven safe, or permit them until proven harmful?*

**FC-12.1 (P)**
> **A:** When a powerful new technology carries uncertain risks, it should not be deployed at scale until those risks are well understood — some harms, once done, cannot be undone, and caution is the only responsible default.
>
> **B:** When a powerful new technology could produce enormous benefits but carries uncertain risks, society should move forward and correct problems as they emerge — excessive caution has costs too, and delay itself causes suffering by withholding solutions to known problems.

**FC-12.2 (I)**
> **A:** The burden of proof should fall on those who develop powerful new technologies — they must demonstrate safety before deployment, just as pharmaceutical companies must prove drugs are safe before they reach patients.
>
> **B:** The burden of proof should fall on those who want to restrict new technologies — innovation should proceed by default, and regulation should respond to demonstrated harms rather than hypothetical ones.

**FC-12.3 (S)**
> **A:** If an AI system could dramatically improve government decision-making but its internal reasoning is not fully explainable, the government should not deploy it — decisions affecting people's lives must be made through processes that can be understood and challenged.
>
> **B:** If an AI system could dramatically improve government decision-making, the government should deploy it even if its reasoning isn't fully explainable — the quality of outcomes matters more than whether the process is transparent, especially if the alternative is worse human decision-making.

## THE CHANCELLOR'S BUDGET — Resource Allocation Exercise

### Setup

> You are the head of state of a mid-sized nation. Your government's total annual budget is **100 units**. You must allocate these units across 10 ministries.
>
> Each ministry requires a **minimum of 5 units** to function at a basic level. This means 50 units are automatically committed. You have **50 discretionary units** to distribute according to your priorities.
>
> There are no right answers. Every allocation reflects a tradeoff — funding one priority means less for another.

### Ministries

**1. Social Welfare & Public Health**
Provides cash transfers to those in poverty, pensions for the elderly, disability support, public hospitals and clinics, epidemic preparedness, and maternal/child health programs.

**2. Economic Development & Trade**
Funds business incentives, trade agreement negotiation, workforce retraining, industrial policy, small business support, and management of trade relationships with other nations.

**3. Ecological Transition & Conservation**
Funds emissions reduction programs, habitat and biodiversity protection, renewable energy development, pollution monitoring and enforcement, and climate adaptation infrastructure.

**4. Defense & Military**
Maintains armed forces, weapons systems, military research and development, veterans' support, and border defense infrastructure.

**5. Domestic Security & Law Enforcement**
Funds police forces, criminal courts, prison systems, border immigration control, counter-terrorism, and public surveillance systems.

**6. Education & Research**
Funds public schools, universities, vocational training, basic scientific research, public media and libraries, and adult education programs.

**7. Cultural Heritage & National Identity**
Supports historic site preservation, language and cultural programs, religious institution partnerships, national arts funding, public monuments, and civic ceremonies.

**8. Infrastructure & Technology**
Builds and maintains roads, rail, power grids, water systems, broadband networks, digital government platforms, and civilian technology research.

**9. Foreign Affairs & International Cooperation**
Funds embassies and diplomatic staff, foreign development aid, contributions to international organizations, treaty negotiations, and humanitarian assistance abroad.

**10. Civil Liberties & Judicial Independence**
Funds constitutional courts, independent judicial oversight, civil rights enforcement, privacy protection agencies, legal aid for citizens, election administration, and government transparency offices.

### Contradiction Detection

After the budget exercise is completed, the scoring engine compares the budget allocation pattern against the respondent's forced-choice and scaled item responses for each axis. The system flags any case where:

- A respondent's forced-choice responses consistently favor Pole A on an axis, but their budget allocation scores strongly toward Pole B (or vice versa)
- The magnitude of discrepancy exceeds a defined threshold (suggested: > 1.5 standard deviations between stated and revealed preference scores)

These contradictions are reported to the respondent as **"Tensions"** — areas where their principles and practical priorities diverge. This is framed as informative, not as an error.

## Implementation Notes

### Question Ordering

- **Phase 1 — Forced-Choice Dilemmas (FC and PT items):** All 36 forced-choice items, randomized across axes. This phase moves quickly and establishes directional preferences.
- **Phase 2 — Calibrated Scales (SC items):** All 36 scaled items, randomized across axes. This phase captures intensity and nuance.
- **Phase 3 — The Chancellor's Budget:** The resource allocation exercise. Placed last because it benefits from the respondent having already reflected on their values through Phases 1 and 2.

### Randomization

- Within each phase, question order should be fully randomized for each respondent.
- For forced-choice items, which statement appears as "A" vs. "B" (i.e., top vs. bottom) should be randomized per respondent to avoid position bias.

### Keying Balance

- Across the full item set, approximately half of items are "keyed" toward each pole — i.e., the socially desirable or "obviously correct" answer (if one exists) points toward Pole A in roughly half the items and Pole B in the other half.
- This is already reflected in the question design above, but should be verified during any revision of the item bank.
