# The Governance Compass — Inline Glossary

## How to Use This Document

This is the complete term list for the inline glossary tooltip feature. Each entry includes:

- **Term** — the word or phrase as it appears (or should be tagged) in the question text
- **Definition** — plain-language explanation at roughly a 7th-grade reading level, written conversationally
- **Wikipedia** — link for the "Learn more →" tooltip footer
- **Appears in** — which questions use or imply this term (for implementation reference)

### Annotation Guidelines

- Tag **0–3 terms per question** on average. Not every question needs annotations.
- Only tag a term **the first time it appears** in a given question. If "sovereignty" appears in both the headline and the body, tag it in the headline only.
- If a term appears in **multiple questions**, tag it every time — the user may encounter questions in random order.
- Don't tag common words a reasonably educated adult would know ("government," "elections," "military," "taxes").
- When in doubt, ask: "Would Erika pause on this word, even for a second?" If yes, tag it.

## Axis & Pole Label Terms

These appear in axis labels, pole labels, domain headers, and results pages — not just in questions.

### 1. Sovereignty

**Definition:** A country's right to govern itself and make its own decisions without being controlled by other countries or outside organizations.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereignty

**Appears in:** Axis 10 pole label, FC-10.1, FC-10.2, FC-10.3, SC-10.1, FC-11.3, results page axis labels

---

### 2. Subsidiarity

**Definition:** The idea that decisions should be made by the smallest or most local group that can handle them. A city shouldn't have to wait for the national government to fix a pothole.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subsidiarity

**Appears in:** Axis 3 description, methodology page

---

### 3. Constructivism (social/political)

**Definition:** The belief that most of human behavior and social patterns are shaped by culture and upbringing — not hardwired — and can be changed by changing the environment.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_constructionism

**Appears in:** Axis 9 pole label, FC-9.1, SC-9.1, results page axis labels

---

### 4. Essentialism

**Definition:** The belief that people have a basic, built-in nature that doesn't change much. Policy should work with human nature rather than trying to redesign it.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essentialism

**Appears in:** Axis 9 pole label, FC-9.1, SC-9.1, results page axis labels

---

### 5. Pluralism

**Definition:** A society where many different groups, beliefs, and ways of life coexist and share power — no single group dominates.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluralism_(political_philosophy)

**Appears in:** Axis 8 pole label, FC-8.1, SC-8.1, results page axis labels

---

### 6. Cohesion (social)

**Definition:** The sense of togetherness and shared identity that holds a society together — common values, language, and culture that make people feel they belong to the same group.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_cohesion

**Appears in:** Axis 8 pole label, FC-8.1, FC-8.2, SC-8.3, results page axis labels

---

### 7. Internationalism

**Definition:** The belief that countries should work closely together, share responsibilities, and sometimes give up some of their independence to solve problems that cross borders.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internationalism_(politics)

**Appears in:** Axis 10 pole label, FC-10.1, SC-10.1, results page axis labels

---

### 8. Interventionism (military)

**Definition:** The belief that a country should be willing to use its military in other countries — to protect allies, stop violence, or keep global order.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interventionism_(politics)

**Appears in:** Axis 11 pole label, FC-11.1, SC-11.1, results page axis labels

---

### 9. Non-interventionism

**Definition:** The belief that a country should not send its military into other countries' conflicts, even when bad things are happening there. Focus on defense only.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-interventionism

**Appears in:** Axis 11 pole label, results page axis labels

---

### 10. Precautionary (principle)

**Definition:** The idea that when a new technology or action might cause serious harm, it's better to hold off until we're sure it's safe — even if that means missing out on potential benefits.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precautionary_principle

**Appears in:** Axis 12 pole label, FC-12.1, SC-12.1, results page axis labels

---

### 11. Popular sovereignty

**Definition:** The principle that the government's power comes from the people, and ordinary citizens should have the final say on how they're governed.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_sovereignty

**Appears in:** Axis 4 pole label, SC-4.1, results page axis labels

---

### 12. Institutional authority

**Definition:** The idea that trained experts and established organizations (courts, agencies, universities) should have significant power to make decisions, because governing is complex.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technocracy

**Appears in:** Axis 4 pole label, SC-4.1, results page axis labels

---

## Economic Terms

### 13. Redistribution

**Definition:** When a government takes money (through taxes) from people who have more and uses it to help people who have less — through programs like public housing, healthcare, or welfare.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redistribution_of_income_and_wealth

**Appears in:** FC-1.1 body, SC-1.1, methodology page

---

### 14. Progressive taxation

**Definition:** A tax system where people who earn more money pay a higher percentage of their income in taxes. The more you make, the bigger your share.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressive_tax

**Appears in:** SC-1.3

---

### 15. Nationalize / Nationalization

**Definition:** When a government takes ownership of a private company or industry, turning it into a publicly-owned operation. The opposite of privatization.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nationalization

**Appears in:** FC-1.3 body

---

### 16. Market allocation

**Definition:** Letting supply and demand — rather than the government — decide how goods, services, and resources get distributed. Basically, letting the market sort things out.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_economy

**Appears in:** Axis 1 pole label, results page

---

### 17. Collective provision

**Definition:** When the government provides services (like healthcare, education, or housing) to everyone, funded by taxes — rather than having people buy them individually.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_good_(economics)

**Appears in:** Axis 1 pole label, results page

---

### 18. GDP (Gross Domestic Product)

**Definition:** A number that measures the total value of everything a country produces in a year. Governments often use it to judge whether the economy is doing well.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product

**Appears in:** FC-2.2 headline

---

### 19. Welfare system / Safety net

**Definition:** Government programs that help people who are struggling — things like unemployment benefits, food assistance, disability support, and housing aid.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welfare_state

**Appears in:** SC-1.1, FC-1.2

---

### 20. Regulatory oversight

**Definition:** When the government watches over businesses and industries to make sure they follow rules — about safety, fairness, environmental protection, etc.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulatory_agency

**Appears in:** SC-1.2, FC-12.2

---

### 21. Ecological boundaries / Ecological limits

**Definition:** The natural limits of what Earth can handle — how much pollution, resource use, and environmental damage the planet can absorb before things break down.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_boundaries

**Appears in:** FC-2.1 body, Axis 2 pole label

---

## Governance & Power Terms

### 22. Referendum / Referenda

**Definition:** A vote where all citizens get to decide directly on a specific issue or law — instead of leaving it to elected representatives. "Referenda" is the plural.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Referendum

**Appears in:** FC-4.2 headline, SC-4.2

---

### 23. Civil liberties

**Definition:** Rights that protect individual freedom from government interference — like freedom of speech, the right to privacy, freedom of religion, and the right to protest.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_liberties

**Appears in:** FC-5.1, FC-5.2, SC-5.2, Chancellor's Budget (Ministry of Civil Liberties)

---

### 24. Constitutional rights

**Definition:** Freedoms and protections written into a country's constitution — the highest law of the land — that the government is not supposed to take away.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_right

**Appears in:** FC-5.2 headline

---

### 25. Surveillance

**Definition:** When a government monitors people's activities, communications, or movements — watching what they do, usually justified by security concerns.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_surveillance

**Appears in:** FC-5.3 headline, SC-5.1

---

### 26. Electoral legitimacy

**Definition:** The idea that a government has the right to rule because the people chose it through free and fair elections.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legitimacy_(political)

**Appears in:** PT-6.1, FC-6.3, SC-6.1

---

### 27. Performance legitimacy

**Definition:** The idea that a government earns the right to rule by delivering good results — prosperity, safety, effective services — regardless of how it came to power.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Performance_legitimacy

**Appears in:** PT-6.1, FC-6.3, SC-6.1, Axis 6 pole label

---

### 28. Devolution

**Definition:** Transferring power from a central (national) government to regional or local governments — letting communities make more of their own decisions.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devolution

**Appears in:** Axis 3 description, SC-3.1

---

### 29. Federalism / Federated structure

**Definition:** A system where power is split between a central government and regional governments (like states or provinces), each with their own authority over certain things.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalism

**Appears in:** Axis 3 description, methodology page

---

## Society & Identity Terms

### 30. Social hierarchy

**Definition:** The way a society ranks people — who has more power, wealth, or status than others. Think of it as the ladder of a society.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_hierarchy

**Appears in:** SC-9.3

---

### 31. Structural barriers

**Definition:** Obstacles built into the way a system works — not individual prejudice, but rules, patterns, and traditions in institutions that make it harder for some groups to succeed.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_inequality

**Appears in:** FC-9.2 headline, SC-9.2

---

### 32. Civic norms

**Definition:** The shared rules and expectations about how people should behave in public life — things like respecting laws, participating in your community, and treating others fairly.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civic_virtue

**Appears in:** FC-8.2 headline/body, PT-8.3

---

### 33. Cultural diversity

**Definition:** Having many different cultures, ethnic groups, languages, and traditions within the same society.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_diversity

**Appears in:** FC-8.1 body, SC-8.1, SC-8.2

---

## International & Security Terms

### 34. Multilateral agreements

**Definition:** Deals or treaties that involve three or more countries agreeing to follow the same rules — like international climate agreements or trade deals.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilateralism

**Appears in:** FC-10.1 body, FC-10.2

---

### 35. Sanctions

**Definition:** Punishments one country (or a group of countries) imposes on another — usually by restricting trade or freezing money — to pressure them into changing their behavior.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_sanctions

**Appears in:** FC-11.3 body

---

### 36. Deterrence

**Definition:** Preventing someone from attacking by making sure they know the consequences would be too painful. Countries build strong militaries partly to discourage others from starting a fight.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deterrence_theory

**Appears in:** SC-11.3, FC-11.1 body

---

### 37. Power projection

**Definition:** A country's ability to use its military far from home — sending troops, ships, or aircraft to distant places to protect interests or allies.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_projection

**Appears in:** SC-11.1, FC-11.2

---

### 38. Preemptive action / Preemption

**Definition:** Attacking first to stop a threat before it becomes dangerous — like striking a country's weapons before they can use them, even though they haven't attacked yet.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preemptive_war

**Appears in:** SC-11.3

---

### 39. Humanitarian intervention

**Definition:** When outside countries step in — sometimes with military force — to stop a government from causing mass suffering to its own people.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanitarian_intervention

**Appears in:** FC-11.3, SC-11.2

---

### 40. Atrocities

**Definition:** Extremely cruel and violent acts — like massacres, torture, or other serious crimes against large groups of people — often committed by a government against its own citizens.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_atrocity_crimes

**Appears in:** FC-11.3 headline/body

---

## Technology & Innovation Terms

### 41. Autonomous systems

**Definition:** Machines or software that can make decisions and take actions on their own, without a human controlling them — like self-driving cars or automated weapons.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomous_robot

**Appears in:** SC-12.2 question stem, Axis 12 description

---

### 42. Burden of proof

**Definition:** Who has to provide evidence in a disagreement. If the "burden of proof" falls on innovators, they have to prove their technology is safe. If it falls on regulators, they have to prove it's dangerous.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burden_of_proof_(philosophy)

**Appears in:** FC-12.2, SC-12.1

---

### 43. Genetic engineering

**Definition:** Changing an organism's DNA on purpose — to cure diseases, improve crops, or (controversially) alter human traits. Powerful but raises big ethical questions.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_engineering

**Appears in:** SC-12.2 question stem, SC-12.3

---

## Chancellor's Budget Terms

These appear in the Phase 3 budget allocation exercise.

### 44. Public infrastructure

**Definition:** The basic physical systems a society needs to function — roads, bridges, water systems, power grids, internet networks, public transportation.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrastructure

**Appears in:** Chancellor's Budget — Ministry of Infrastructure

---

### 45. Diplomatic corps / Foreign affairs

**Definition:** The government department and its staff responsible for managing a country's relationships with other countries — ambassadors, treaties, negotiations.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomacy

**Appears in:** Chancellor's Budget — Ministry of Foreign Affairs

---

### 46. Cultural heritage

**Definition:** The traditions, art, language, historical sites, and customs passed down through generations that define a group's identity and history.

**Wikipedia:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_heritage

**Appears in:** Chancellor's Budget — Ministry of Cultural Heritage
