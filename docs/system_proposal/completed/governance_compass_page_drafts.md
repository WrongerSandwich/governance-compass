## Why existing political quizzes fall short

If you've ever taken a political quiz online, you've probably had the experience of getting a result that felt partially right and partially absurd. You land somewhere on a 2D grid and think: "Sure, I'm roughly there on economics, but this completely misses how I feel about surveillance, or immigration, or whether experts or ordinary people should drive policy." The result captures a shadow of your worldview — recognizable in outline, missing in substance.

This isn't a failure of execution. It's a failure of architecture. The dominant model for mapping political beliefs — a two-dimensional plane with an economic axis and a social axis — was a genuine breakthrough when it emerged. It showed millions of people that politics has more than one dimension, which is a real insight. But two dimensions cannot represent the full space of governance philosophy any more than latitude and longitude can describe the shape of a mountain. You need elevation data. You need contour lines.

The problem goes deeper than dimensionality. Nearly every popular political quiz asks you to rate your agreement with a series of statements: "Strongly agree, agree, disagree, strongly disagree." This format has a well-documented flaw called acquiescence bias — the tendency to agree with whatever statement is put in front of you, regardless of content. Research has shown this bias can inflate attitude measurements by up to 50% and in some cases reverse the apparent direction of a person's beliefs. When you "agree" with a statement about government regulation and then "agree" with a statement about free markets, the quiz has no way to tell whether you hold a genuinely nuanced middle position or whether you were just agreeing with things that sounded reasonable.

And then there's the problem of cultural portability. Most quizzes embed assumptions about Western democratic institutions so deeply that they become invisible to their designers. The word "liberal" means free-market in Australia and progressive in America. "Free speech" carries First Amendment connotations that don't exist in countries where expression is constitutionally balanced against dignity or social harmony. Questions about gun control, healthcare systems, or immigration policy assume specific institutional contexts that billions of people don't share. A quiz that only works in one political culture isn't measuring governance philosophy — it's measuring where you sit within a particular national debate.

None of this means existing quizzes are worthless. The Political Compass introduced the two-axis model to a mass audience. 8values and 9axes expanded the dimensional space. ISideWith made issue-based candidate matching practical. The Pew Research Center's Political Typology set a gold standard for methodological rigor. The Governance Compass builds on what all of them started — and tries to address what they left unfinished.

## Twelve dimensions, not two

Political science has spent decades studying the structure of political ideology, and the research converges on a few key findings. First, there are at minimum two robust, independent dimensions: an economic dimension (attitudes toward redistribution, state intervention, and market freedom) and a cultural dimension (attitudes toward tradition, authority, social change, and individual autonomy). These two dimensions appear in virtually every major framework — the Chapel Hill Expert Survey's economic left-right and GAL-TAN axes, the World Values Survey's Survival/Self-Expression and Traditional/Secular-Rational dimensions, the Moral Foundations Theory's individualizing and binding foundations.

But two dimensions are a validated floor, not a ceiling. As political scientists have expanded their measurement, additional meaningful axes have emerged that don't collapse onto the primary two. Populism versus institutional trust has become one of the most important political cleavages globally — and it's orthogonal to both the economic and cultural dimensions. Attitudes toward international cooperation versus national sovereignty structure party competition across Europe in ways that the economic axis alone can't explain. Attitudes toward emerging technology governance — how much precaution is appropriate when powerful new capabilities carry uncertain risks — are rapidly becoming a structuring political question with no natural home on a traditional left-right spectrum.

The Governance Compass measures 12 dimensions organized into four domains: Economic Organization (2 axes), Power and Authority (4 axes), Society and Identity (3 axes), and The State in the World (3 axes). This count reflects a deliberate balance. Fewer axes would sacrifice distinctions that matter — collapsing populism into the cultural dimension, or merging interventionism with internationalism, loses real information. More axes would introduce redundancy and respondent fatigue without adding discriminant validity. Several additional dimensions were considered during design — rehabilitative versus punitive justice, transhumanism versus bioconservatism — and excluded because they loaded onto existing axes in theoretical analysis or were too narrow for a general-purpose instrument.

For quick visual comparison, the 12 axes are also reduced to two super-dimensions that produce a familiar compass-style plot. But the compass plot is always presented as a simplification — a concession to shareability, not the real result. The full 12-axis radar chart is the instrument's primary output.

## Three ways of asking

The Governance Compass doesn't just ask more questions than a typical political quiz — it asks them differently. Three distinct question formats are used, each designed to capture something the others can't.

**Forced-choice tradeoffs** form the backbone of the assessment. Each of the 36 forced-choice items presents two statements representing opposite poles of a single axis. The respondent must choose which statement comes closer to their view. There is no neutral option, no "both," and no scale.

This format exists to eliminate acquiescence bias. When there's no "agree" button, you can't default to agreeing — you have to choose between two positions that are both designed to sound reasonable. The format also mirrors how governance actually works: political decisions are almost never about whether something is good or bad in a vacuum. They're about which competing good to prioritize when you can't have both.

Each axis has forced-choice items at three levels of abstraction — a philosophical principle, an institutional design question, and a concrete applied scenario. This tests whether a respondent's commitment to a value holds when it moves from the abstract to the specific. Someone who endorses liberty as a principle but supports broad surveillance in a concrete scenario has revealed something important about the boundaries of their commitment.

This approach draws on the methodology of the Pew Research Center's Political Typology, which uses forced binary choice between opposing statements and is the most methodologically rigorous widely-used political assessment tool.

**Calibrated scales** complement the forced-choice items by capturing intensity. The 36 scaled items each pose a question with five response options, but unlike most quizzes, the response options are specific to each question — not generic "agree/disagree" anchors. A question about how much authority local communities should have offers a scale from "near-total" to "almost none," with each option describing a concrete governance arrangement. The response options themselves communicate the spectrum, making the gradient visible at a glance.

**The Chancellor's Budget** is a single interactive exercise that captures something no questionnaire can: revealed preferences under scarcity. The respondent allocates 100 units of national budget across 10 government ministries. Each ministry needs a minimum of 5 units to function. There isn't enough to fully fund everything — that's the point.

A person who claims to prioritize environmental protection in the abstract but, when forced to allocate scarce resources, consistently funds economic growth over ecological transition has revealed something their questionnaire answers obscured. The budget exercise surfaces these priority hierarchies because it imposes the fundamental constraint of real governance: opportunity cost. Funding one thing means defunding another.

## How scoring works

Each of the 12 axes produces a final score from -1.0 (strong Pole A) to +1.0 (strong Pole B), with 0.0 representing genuine ambivalence or a balanced position.

The score is a weighted composite of the three modalities. Forced-choice responses establish the directional preference and receive the highest weight (40%). Scaled responses capture intensity and nuance (35%). The budget allocation reveals operational priorities (25%). These weights reflect a judgment about what each modality measures best: forced-choice is most resistant to bias, scales capture gradation most precisely, and the budget captures something qualitatively different from either.

The weights vary by axis depending on how directly the budget exercise maps to that axis. Some axes — like the economic model, where Social Welfare and Economic Development ministries map directly to the two poles — get full budget weighting. Others — like Constructivism versus Essentialism, where no ministry cleanly captures the distinction — receive their scores entirely from the questionnaire modalities.

One of the instrument's most distinctive features is its contradiction detection system. After scoring, the algorithm compares each axis's stated preference (from forced-choice and scaled items) against its revealed preference (from budget allocation). When these diverge significantly — when you say you value one thing but fund another — the system flags the discrepancy as a "tension."

Tensions are reported to the respondent as informative features, not errors. Holding contradictory commitments is normal. It's also genuinely useful to know about. The tension between what you believe in principle and what you prioritize in practice is often where the most interesting self-knowledge lives.

The 12 axis scores are also reduced to two super-dimensions for the compass plot: an Economic composite (weighted primarily by Axis 1 with contribution from Axis 2) and a Cultural-Authority composite (weighted primarily by Axis 7 with contributions from Axes 4, 5, 8, and 9). These super-dimensions correspond to the two primary factors consistently identified in political science research.

Finally, the 12-axis profile is matched against a set of governance archetype prototypes — idealized profiles representing coherent governance philosophies like "Civic Institutionalist," "Developmental Modernizer," or "Green Communalist." The respondent is assigned to the closest archetype and shown their degree of match, their second-closest archetype, and a description of each archetype's internal logic and characteristic tensions. The current prototypes are rule-based, designed from governance theory. As more people take the assessment, they will be replaced with empirically derived clusters.

The scoring does not tell you who to vote for. It does not assume any pole of any axis is the correct answer. It does not pathologize any position. It shows you where you stand, how consistently you stand there, and what governance tradition your overall profile most resembles.

## Designing for cross-cultural validity

The Governance Compass is built to work the same way regardless of the respondent's country, language, or political system. This isn't just a matter of avoiding country-specific policy references — it requires rethinking how questions are framed at a fundamental level.

No question in the instrument references a specific country, political leader, party, policy by name, constitutional provision, or institutional structure. Concepts that carry specific cultural freight — "liberal," "conservative," "left," "right," "free speech," "democracy" as a monolithic concept — never appear in question text. Each is decomposed into its underlying governance tension. "Free speech" becomes a question about whether the state should restrict expression when it may incite violence or undermine stability. "Democracy" is separated into its component features: electoral competition, citizen participation, institutional accountability, judicial independence.

For culturally sensitive items, the instrument uses the portrait method, adapted from Schwartz's Portrait Values Questionnaire. Rather than asking "Do you believe X?" — which triggers social desirability bias in contexts where X is stigmatized — the question describes a hypothetical person: "This person believes that a government's right to govern comes from results, not elections. How similar is this person to you?" This framing gives respondents permission to identify with positions that might feel uncomfortable to endorse directly.

Most critically, the instrument includes Axis 6 (Electoral Process ↔ Performance Outcomes), which explicitly treats non-democratic governance as a coherent position rather than a failure state. This axis is absent from every other popular political quiz, and its absence is what makes those quizzes Western-centric at a structural level. In much of the world — developmental states in East Asia, monarchies in the Gulf, one-party states across several continents — political legitimacy derives primarily from what a government delivers, not from how it was selected. An instrument that measures governance philosophy without accommodating this reality isn't country-agnostic — it's just measuring how democratic you are on a scale that assumes democracy is the only valid answer.

## Limitations and what comes next

Honesty about limitations is more credible than overclaiming, so here's what this instrument is and isn't.

The Governance Compass is a v1 instrument. Its dimensional structure is grounded in political science theory and comparative governance research, but it has not undergone formal psychometric validation. The 12 axes have not been confirmed through exploratory factor analysis on response data. Test-retest reliability has not been measured. Cross-cultural measurement invariance — the statistical confirmation that the same constructs are being measured across different populations — has not been tested.

The archetype prototypes are hand-crafted from governance theory, not derived from empirical clustering of real responses. The scoring weights are based on methodological reasoning about what each modality captures, not on empirical optimization.

This matters. It means the instrument should be used as a tool for structured self-reflection — a way to think about your governance philosophy across more dimensions than you've probably considered — not as a scientific diagnostic or a validated psychometric measure.

The roadmap toward greater rigor is concrete. As response data accumulates: internal consistency testing to verify that items within each axis measure the same construct. Exploratory factor analysis to confirm the 12-axis structure emerges from the data. Test-retest reliability assessment to measure score stability over time. And ultimately, multi-group measurement invariance testing with respondents from diverse cultural contexts.

If you're a researcher with expertise in psychometrics, political psychology, or comparative politics and you're interested in collaborating on validation work, the project welcomes that conversation.

# Page 2: The twelve axes

**URL:** `/axes`

---

The Governance Compass measures your political philosophy across twelve independent dimensions, organized into four domains. Each axis represents a genuine tension in how human societies can be organized — not a right answer and a wrong answer, but two defensible priorities that pull in different directions.

Your position on one axis does not determine your position on another. A person can favor collective economic provision while opposing centralized political authority, or support cultural tradition while embracing technological innovation. The axes are designed to be independent precisely because real political belief systems are more complex than any single spectrum can capture.

## Economic organization

### Axis 1: Collective provision ↔ Market allocation

*What role should organized society play in determining economic outcomes and ensuring material welfare?*

The collective provision pole holds that organized society bears responsibility for ensuring no one falls below a dignified standard of living. It favors redistribution, public services, and constraints on the accumulation of private economic power — not out of hostility toward individual achievement, but from the conviction that unregulated markets produce inequalities that are neither efficient nor just. Real-world governance traditions at this pole include Nordic social democracy, postwar European welfare states, and socialist economic planning.

The market allocation pole holds that voluntary exchange, private ownership, and price signals produce better outcomes than central direction. It favors minimal state intervention in the economy — not from indifference to poverty, but from the conviction that market mechanisms generate prosperity more reliably than bureaucratic allocation, and that the freedom to keep what you earn is a fundamental right. Real-world governance traditions at this pole include laissez-faire capitalism, free-trade city-states like historical Hong Kong, and supply-side economic policy.

This is the single most empirically robust dimension in political science. It appears in every major framework for mapping political ideology and consistently emerges as the primary factor in analyses of political attitudes worldwide.

### Axis 2: Ecological limits ↔ Growth imperative

*Is continuous economic expansion compatible with a finite planet, and should governance prioritize sustainability over prosperity?*

The ecological limits pole holds that there are hard physical boundaries to how much humanity can produce and consume. It argues that governance must prioritize sustainability, sufficiency, and ecological health — even if this means accepting less material wealth or deliberately reducing aggregate economic output. This isn't anti-prosperity; it's the conviction that genuine prosperity requires a livable planet, and that infinite growth on a finite world is a mathematical impossibility.

The growth imperative pole holds that economic expansion remains essential for human flourishing and that innovation can decouple prosperity from environmental degradation. It argues that growth is not the enemy of sustainability — it's the mechanism that funds clean technology, lifts people out of poverty, and provides the resources to address environmental problems. Shrinking the economy would make every challenge harder, not easier.

This axis is independent from Axis 1. A committed socialist and a committed capitalist can both be productivists. A degrowth advocate and a green-technology optimist can both care deeply about the environment but disagree fundamentally about whether limits are necessary. This is the axis most likely to surprise people who assume environmental concern automatically maps onto left-wing economics.

## Power and authority

### Axis 3: Distributed governance ↔ Centralized governance

*How should political and administrative power be distributed across geographic and institutional levels?*

The distributed governance pole favors maximum devolution of power — local autonomy, federated structures, subsidiarity. It holds that the people closest to a problem are best positioned to solve it, and that distant central authorities inevitably produce one-size-fits-all solutions that fit nobody well. This pole values responsiveness, experimentation, and the prevention of concentrated power.

The centralized governance pole favors a strong national authority that imposes uniform standards across all territory. It holds that consistency, efficiency, and fairness require central coordination — without it, regions diverge in ways that create inequality, confusion, and threaten national cohesion. This pole values standardization, coordination, and the ability to address problems that cross regional boundaries.

This axis is independent of whether a state is democratic or autocratic. A centralized democracy (like France) and a centralized autocracy sit on the same pole for different reasons. A federated democracy (like Switzerland) and a decentralized traditional society sit on the other. The axis measures *where* power sits, not *who* holds it.

### Axis 4: Popular sovereignty ↔ Institutional authority

*Should governance be guided primarily by the expressed will of ordinary people, or by the judgment of trained specialists and established institutions?*

The popular sovereignty pole holds that the collective wisdom of ordinary people — grounded in lived experience, common sense, and practical knowledge — is a more trustworthy guide to good governance than the theories of credentialed experts. It favors direct participation, referenda, recall elections, and deep skepticism of elite institutions. It argues that experts are frequently disconnected from the realities most people face, and that concentrating decision-making power in credentialed hands is itself a form of disenfranchisement.

The institutional authority pole holds that modern governance is too complex for popular intuition. It favors delegation to trained specialists, established bureaucratic institutions, and evidence-based policy processes. It argues that good governance requires knowledge that most citizens don't have time to acquire, and that insulating technical decisions from popular pressure produces better outcomes — not because ordinary people are foolish, but because governing a complex society is genuinely difficult.

This is the populism-technocracy axis, and it has become one of the most important political cleavages worldwide. Both poles can be democratic: a populist democrat wants more referenda and direct participation; a technocratic democrat wants elected leaders to delegate effectively to qualified experts. The axis is about the *locus* of decision-making, not whether the system is democratic.

### Axis 5: Liberty ↔ Security

*When individual freedom and collective safety conflict, which should the state prioritize?*

The liberty pole demands strict limits on state power. It prioritizes individual privacy, freedom of expression, robust constraints on surveillance and policing, and strong constitutional protections against government overreach — even if these protections occasionally allow threats to go undetected. It holds that a state powerful enough to protect you from every danger is powerful enough to become the danger, and that history overwhelmingly supports this concern.

The security pole prioritizes the state's duty to protect its people. It endorses robust law enforcement, surveillance capabilities, and decisive intervention against threats — even at the cost of some individual privacy and autonomy. It holds that safety is the precondition for all other freedoms: a society where people live in fear of crime or violence is not free in any meaningful sense, regardless of what rights are written in its constitution.

This is the Hobbesian tradeoff — one of the oldest and most persistent tensions in political philosophy. Real-world debates that activate this axis include post-9/11 surveillance policy, pandemic-era lockdown disputes, and the ongoing tension between encryption/privacy and law enforcement access to communications.

### Axis 6: Electoral process ↔ Performance outcomes

*Does a government's legitimacy come primarily from how it was chosen, or from what it delivers?*

The electoral process pole holds that a government's right to govern derives from the free consent of the governed. Elections, rule of law, peaceful transfer of power, and institutional accountability are not just useful mechanisms — they are the *source* of legitimate authority. A government that was not freely chosen is never fully legitimate, no matter how well it performs. The process of choosing is sacred.

The performance outcomes pole holds that a government's right to govern derives from results. A government that delivers sustained prosperity, personal safety, effective public services, and national dignity has earned its authority — regardless of how its leaders were selected. People need good governance more than they need to have chosen their governors, and the track record of electoral democracy in delivering good governance is more mixed than its advocates admit.

This axis is the most important for cross-cultural validity, and it is absent from every other popular political quiz. Its absence is what makes most political assessments structurally Western-centric. In much of the world — developmental states in East Asia, Gulf monarchies, one-party states across several continents — political legitimacy is primarily performance-based. Including this axis doesn't endorse any particular governance model. It simply measures where people actually stand on a question that billions of people answer differently than Western democratic theory assumes.

## Society and identity

### Axis 7: Progressive change ↔ Continuity and tradition

*Should a society actively reform its inherited norms and institutions, or preserve them as sources of stability and meaning?*

The progressive change pole holds that inherited customs, hierarchies, and institutions should be continuously re-examined in light of new knowledge and evolving moral understanding. What was once accepted is not automatically worth preserving. Social progress is real — moral knowledge advances, just as scientific knowledge does — and institutions should advance with it. Resistance to change, however dressed up in appeals to wisdom and stability, is often just a defense of existing privilege.

The continuity and tradition pole holds that inherited institutions represent the accumulated practical wisdom of generations who faced real challenges. They were not designed by theory; they were tested by survival. Discarding them because they conflict with contemporary intellectual fashion is arrogant and dangerous — the reformer who destroys a structure before understanding why it exists will discover, too late, what it was holding up. Caution is not cowardice; it is respect for complexity.

This is the second of the two empirically robust "super-dimensions" in political science, appearing across every major framework under different names. It's also the axis where people are most likely to feel their position is obviously correct and the other pole is obviously wrong — which is precisely why both poles are described here in their strongest form.

### Axis 8: Pluralism ↔ Cohesion

*When a society contains diverse groups, should the state protect that diversity or cultivate a unified identity?*

The pluralism pole views a society with many distinct cultural traditions, languages, and ways of life as inherently richer and more resilient. It argues that the state should actively protect and accommodate this diversity — providing public services in multiple languages, recognizing diverse cultural practices in law, and ensuring that all communities can maintain their distinct identities while participating fully in civic life. Diversity is not a problem to be managed; it is a source of strength.

The cohesion pole holds that a functional society requires a shared language, a shared set of civic values, and a shared cultural identity. It argues that the state should cultivate common ground — a civic culture that all residents are expected to adopt — because without it, society fractures along group lines into mutual incomprehension and sectarian competition. This isn't hostility toward any group; it's the conviction that "we" must mean something concrete if a society is to hold together.

Both poles believe they're describing the conditions for a stable, flourishing society. They disagree about whether diversity or unity is the more important ingredient. Real-world anchoring: Canada's official multiculturalism sits at one end; France's laïcité and civic universalism sit at the other. Most societies navigate a position somewhere between.

### Axis 9: Constructivism ↔ Essentialism

*Are human behavior and social hierarchies primarily shaped by culture and institutions, or by durable features of human nature?*

The constructivism pole holds that most of what we call "human nature" — including differences in behavior and outcomes between groups — is primarily the product of culture, upbringing, and institutional design. If these are constructed, they can be reconstructed. Change the structures, and you change the outcomes. This view grounds the belief that ambitious social policy can reshape society in fundamental ways, that persistent inequalities prove structural failure, and that the goal of governance should be to actively engineer more equitable outcomes.

The essentialism pole holds that human beings have a durable nature shaped by biology, evolutionary history, or (for some) divine design. Social structures that ignore this nature — however well-intentioned — will fail, because they are fighting against something more fundamental than policy. This doesn't mean accepting injustice; it means designing governance that works with human nature rather than against it, and accepting that some variation in outcomes reflects genuine differences in preferences, aptitudes, and values rather than systemic failure.

This is the deepest and most philosophical axis in the instrument. It sits underneath many of the other axes — your position here influences how you think about economic inequality (Axis 1), social change (Axis 7), and group outcomes (Axis 8). Essentialism here does not mean crude biological determinism. It includes anyone who believes human nature meaningfully constrains what policy can achieve, whether they ground that belief in evolutionary psychology, religious anthropology, or practical experience.

## The state in the world

### Axis 10: Internationalism ↔ Sovereignty

*Should the state participate deeply in international cooperation, or prioritize its own independence and self-determination?*

The internationalism pole holds that the most serious challenges facing humanity — climate change, pandemics, economic instability, nuclear proliferation — cannot be solved by any single nation acting alone. States must be willing to pool sovereignty, accept binding international agreements, and strengthen global governance institutions — even when those agreements impose costs on individual nations. The alternative is a world where collective action problems go unsolved because no one is willing to bear their share of the cost.

The sovereignty pole holds that a nation's first duty is to its own citizens, and that binding international agreements inevitably transfer decision-making power to distant institutions that are not accountable to the people they affect. Cooperation is valuable when it serves the national interest, but sovereignty — the right to make your own laws, control your own borders, and chart your own course — must not be compromised. The alternative is a world where unelected international bodies override the democratic will of national populations.

The genuine tension is real: international cooperation requires giving up some self-determination, and national sovereignty requires accepting that some global problems go unsolved. Neither position is naive about what it costs.

### Axis 11: Non-interventionism ↔ Interventionism

*Should the state project military force abroad, or commit to strict non-intervention?*

The non-interventionism pole holds that military force — even when used with good intentions — almost always causes more suffering than it prevents. The history of foreign intervention is overwhelmingly one of unintended consequences, civilian casualties, and prolonged instability. A nation should maintain only enough military capability for self-defense and should never deploy forces beyond its own borders. Foreign conflicts are not its responsibility.

The interventionism pole holds that military strength and the credible willingness to use it are essential tools of statecraft. A world where aggressive states face no consequences is more dangerous than one where capable nations enforce a baseline of order. Defending allies, protecting trade routes, deterring aggression, and — in extreme cases — stopping atrocities are legitimate uses of military power. The alternative is not peace; it's a world where the strong do what they will and the weak suffer what they must.

This axis is independent from Axis 10. A nation can be deeply engaged in international institutions while opposing military intervention, or sovereignty-focused while maintaining a powerful military. The axis measures attitudes toward hard power projection specifically, not international engagement in general.

### Axis 12: Precautionary ↔ Innovation-first

*When powerful new technologies carry uncertain risks, should the state restrict them until proven safe, or permit them until proven harmful?*

The precautionary pole holds that when a powerful technology carries uncertain risks — particularly risks that could be catastrophic or irreversible — it should not be deployed at scale until those risks are well understood. The burden of proof falls on innovators to demonstrate safety, not on regulators to demonstrate danger. Some harms, once done, cannot be undone. Caution is the only responsible default when the stakes include existential risk.

The innovation-first pole holds that technological progress is humanity's most powerful tool for solving problems, and that excessive caution has costs too. Delay itself causes suffering — every year a beneficial technology is held back is a year its benefits are denied to people who need them. Regulation should respond to demonstrated harms, not hypothetical ones. The burden of proof should fall on those who want to restrict, not on those who want to build.

This axis becomes more important with every passing year. AI governance, genetic engineering, autonomous weapons systems, surveillance technology, synthetic biology — these are all policy domains where the precautionary/innovation tension is the primary structuring question and where it doesn't map cleanly onto traditional left-right politics. Real-world anchoring: the EU's AI Act represents the precautionary pole; regulatory approaches that emphasize permissionless innovation represent the other.

---

No axis has a correct answer. The value of the Governance Compass is not in telling you which pole is right — it's in showing you where you stand across all twelve, where your commitments are strong, where you're genuinely ambivalent, and where your positions connect to each other in ways you might not have expected. The full profile is more than the sum of its parts.

## Why this exists

I built The Governance Compass because I wanted to see my own political beliefs clearly.

Not "which party do I side with" or "where do I land on the left-right spectrum" — those questions have easy answers and they're the wrong questions. I wanted to understand the shape of my actual political philosophy: what I believe about how human societies should be organized, where my commitments are consistent, and where they quietly contradict each other.

Modern political life pushes you to pick a team, adopt its platform wholesale, and defend positions you've never examined. The two-party framework, the left-right spectrum, the social media dynamics that reward tribal loyalty over genuine reasoning — all of it conspires to flatten complex human worldviews into simple team affiliations. I suspect most people, if they sat down and honestly mapped their beliefs across enough dimensions, would find they don't fit neatly into any existing political category. They'd find something more interesting: a personal political philosophy with its own internal logic, its own tensions, and its own surprises.

I wanted a tool that would surface that. And when I went looking for one, I couldn't find it.

## What I found and what was missing

I took the Political Compass and got a dot on a 2×2 grid. It captured maybe a third of what I care about. I tried 8values, 9axes, PolitiScales — more dimensions, which helped, but the same agree/disagree format that let me say yes to everything without making hard choices. I could agree that the environment matters AND that economic growth is essential AND that the state should stay out of people's lives AND that security requires strong institutions, all without once confronting the tradeoffs between those positions. The quizzes measured my sentiments. They didn't test my priorities.

None of them forced me to allocate scarce resources and discover what I'd actually fund when I couldn't fund everything. None of them told me where my stated principles and my practical priorities diverge. And none of them worked outside a Western democratic framework — they assumed that "democracy" was the only valid answer to the question of political legitimacy, which is a position, not an axiom.

## What I built

The Governance Compass maps your political philosophy across 12 dimensions — from economic organization and the structure of political authority to cultural identity, international relations, and technology governance. It uses three different question formats: forced-choice tradeoffs that make you choose between competing goods, calibrated scales that capture intensity, and a budget allocation exercise that reveals what you actually prioritize when resources are scarce.

It's country-agnostic by design. No questions reference specific countries, parties, leaders, or policies. Instead of asking "Do you support universal healthcare?" it asks "Should organized society guarantee that people receive medical treatment regardless of ability to pay?" The difference matters — the first question measures your position in a specific national debate; the second measures your underlying governance principle. A respondent in Stockholm and a respondent in Lagos are answering the same question about the same human tension.

The instrument doesn't tell you who to vote for. It doesn't tell you whether you're right. It shows you the shape of your own thinking — where you hold strong convictions, where you're genuinely torn, and where your stated values and practical priorities point in different directions.

## What this is and isn't

**It is** a tool for structured self-reflection. A way to see your political worldview mapped with more granularity than you've probably encountered before. A starting point for more honest conversations about governance — conversations where you can point to specific dimensions of agreement and disagreement rather than sorting each other into tribes.

**It isn't** a validated scientific instrument — not yet. The dimensional architecture is grounded in political science research, but the quiz itself hasn't undergone formal psychometric validation. The methodology page describes what that validation would involve and the roadmap for getting there. Use the results as a mirror for your thinking, not as a diagnosis.

**What I hope it becomes:** a project that helps people engage with politics at the level of principles rather than teams. Something that reveals common ground between people who think they disagree about everything, and genuine disagreements between people who think they're on the same side.

## The project

The Governance Compass is a personal project. The scoring algorithm is fully transparent — you can see exactly how every item contributed to every axis score. The project aspires to open-source its question bank, scoring methodology, and any validation data it produces.

There are no ads, no data sales, and no tracking beyond what's needed to display your results. Your responses are processed in your browser. The shareable results link encodes your scores in the URL itself — no server-side storage required.

If you're a researcher, educator, or developer interested in the project, I'd welcome the conversation. And if you take the quiz and learn something about your own thinking that surprises you — that's the whole point.
